NYC Townhouse Stories
Joe is a NYC residential agent building an upmarket townhouse practice. This skill pulls fresh townhouse stories from the press so he has (a) raw material for his newsletter and (b) timely, expert-coded things to mention with prospects.

The goal is a high-signal digest with a point of view, not a link dump. Quality over volume. Better to hand back five stories Joe actually wants to talk about than twelve he has to sort through.
When to use this skill
Trigger on any of:

	•	"What's happening with NYC townhouses?"
	•	"Pull this week's townhouse stories" / "Weekly townhouse digest"
	•	"Anything good for the newsletter this week?"
	•	"Find recent coverage of [trophy sale / restoration / landmark issue / market trend]"
	•	Any request for current townhouse coverage in Manhattan or Brooklyn

If Joe asks something narrower (one neighborhood, one address, one theme), still use this skill — just narrow the search accordingly.
Editorial stance — read this before searching
This is the biggest difference between a useful digest and a useless one.

Who Joe's content is for. The ideal reader is a NYC townhouse buyer, a current townhouse owner, or someone in their orbit (referrer, advisor, family member, friend). Every story should pass the test: would one of those three people find this useful, surprising, or insider-coded?

The model: celebrity hook, expert take. Celebrity coverage is a fine hook — it's how stories travel and how readers find content. The job of the digest is not to filter celebrity stories out; it's to make sure that when Joe uses one, the value underneath the hook is something only an industry insider would offer. Joe's positioning is "give away for free what competitors charge for" — deep insight on the property, the deal mechanics, the neighborhood dynamics, the renovation choices, the price logic, the LPC angle, the broker move.

How to handle the Pink-buys-a-townhouse genre:

	•	Include it. The hook draws people in.
	•	Reframe the headline so the property leads (e.g., "1849 Greek Revival on West 11th trades at $21.5M — and the price tells you more than the buyer does"), with the celebrity as the entry point but not the whole story.
	•	In "Why it's interesting", deliver the insider take that a music news site or a fan account cannot — what the price says about the West Village trophy market, what the discount from ask reveals about current buyer leverage, what the property's Daniel Chester French-era biography contributes to its value, etc.

A celebrity story without an insider take underneath = drop it (it's just gossip). A celebrity story with a genuine insider take = include it with the property leading.

When the question comes up, ask: would a townhouse buyer, owner, or their referrer learn something here that they couldn't get from People.com? If yes, in.

Stories beat data. Generic market snapshots ("Brooklyn median $1.7M, inventory up 0.6%") are not stories — they're charts in the wrong format. If a data point belongs in the digest, it belongs inside a story, anchored to a property, person, or specific deal. Standalone "Market Snapshot" blocks should not appear.

Fair housing discipline. Joe is licensed; the digest is for content production. Avoid framings that could read as steering or as characterizations of protected classes. Also avoid broad demographic generalizations about who buys/owns in a neighborhood, even when not strictly protected — they read like steering even when they aren't, and they erode expert positioning.

	•	Don't characterize a neighborhood by who lives there in protected-class terms — race, religion, national origin, family status (kids/no kids), age, disability, sexual orientation, source of income.
	•	Avoid demographic-by-occupation/lifestyle shorthand too: "creative professionals," "tech bros," "finance types," "theatre people," "academics" — these read as steering even when not protected. If a story actually requires saying who bought, name the specific buyer or use deal-mechanics framing ("a buyer who paid $21.5M all-cash"), not category framing.
	•	"Design-forward buyer," "Trophy buyer," "Investor pool," "All-cash buyer" — fine, those describe behavior or finance.
	•	"Younger buyers," "families with children," "first-generation buyers," "empty nesters" — risky, do not use.
	•	Neighborhood color is fine when it's about the neighborhood (architecture, retail, transit, history). Not fine when it's a stand-in for who's "supposed to" live there.

Two intensities for "Use it as". Not every story needs a deep take. Some stories are quick market-update content — a sentence or two in a newsletter, a quick post, a one-line conversation drop. Others warrant Joe's POV and could anchor a longer piece. The skill should label which, so Joe can plan accordingly.

Multiple angles when warranted. When a story genuinely supports two strong angles (e.g., a deep-take newsletter piece and a quick conversation drop, or two different newsletter angles), give both as separate bullets and let Joe pick. Don't manufacture two angles where there's only one — that's padding. But when both are real, both should be on offer.

Podcast-guest angle (occasional, not required). This is a low-priority, future-leaning idea. If a story features a real person Joe could plausibly invite onto a show one day (the architect on the restoration, the designer behind the interior, the broker on the deal), it's fine to flag it as a separate bullet. Skip it whenever it doesn't fit naturally — it shouldn't appear on most stories.
Inputs
Default assumptions when not specified:

	•	Lookback window: last 21 days, strict. NYC residential real estate moves slowly — a story from three weeks ago is still "current" for newsletter and conversation purposes. If a story was published outside the 21-day window, do not include it unless it's directly relevant context (and label it as such).
	•	Geography: all of NYC (Manhattan + Brooklyn primarily; other boroughs if a story is genuinely notable)
	•	Story types: news/journalism, notable sales/listings, architecture/design/restoration
	•	Story count: 4–8 strong items typical, but quality is the only non-negotiable. A 3-story digest of strong, verified, insider-take pieces is strictly better than a 6-story digest with two weak entries. Never pad to hit a count — it costs Joe his expert positioning. If a window is genuinely slow, say so plainly and ship the strong items only.

If Joe specifies something different (e.g., "last month", "just Brooklyn Heights", "only restoration pieces"), follow that.
Process
1. Read the source list
Read references/sources.md to load the curated sources and search patterns. Work the Tier 1 sources first — they have the highest hit rate.
2. Search
Use web search to find stories from the lookback window. Combine source-targeted searches (site:brownstoner.com townhouse) with broader topical searches (NYC townhouse sale, Brooklyn brownstone restoration, etc.). Don't limit to the curated list — if a strong story shows up from a publication not on the list, include it.

When searching, the current month/year matters — pass the year so results are actually recent.
3. Verify before including
This is non-negotiable. For every story you intend to include:

	•	Open the article with WebFetch. Don't summarize from a search snippet. If WebFetch fails or the URL doesn't resolve, drop the story.
	•	Check the publication date. It must fall in the lookback window. If the article doesn't show a date you can find, treat that as a yellow flag — either confirm the date from another source or drop the story. Do not infer dates.
	•	Verify the specific facts you're going to put in the digest (price, address, name of architect, year built). If the article doesn't actually support a claim, do not include the claim. Better to write less than to write wrong.
	•	If a story is paywalled and you can't read the body, you can include it only if the headline + dek give you enough to summarize honestly. Note the paywall. Do not invent body details.

A digest with three verified stories beats a digest with eight where one is wrong. A wrong story Joe repeats with a prospect or in a newsletter actively damages his positioning.
4. Filter
Apply the editorial stance above and the "what's NOT a townhouse story" filter from references/sources.md. Also drop:

	•	Anything Joe has likely already seen (mainstream story 2+ weeks old that everyone has covered)
	•	Repeats — if three outlets covered the same sale, pick the best-written one and note the others
	•	Pieces with no actual content (PR fluff, single-line listing announcements with no narrative)
	•	Pure celebrity-buyer stories where the property is just a backdrop (see editorial stance)
5. Write the digest
Use the output structure below.
Output structure
Produce a single Markdown digest. Use this exact shape:

# NYC Townhouse Stories — Week of [Monday's date]

_[1–2 sentence framing of the week — what was the dominant thread, if any. Do not pad. If there was no dominant thread, say so plainly.]_

---

## [Story 1 headline — Joe's framing, property-first, 8–12 words]

**Source:** [Publication], [Publication date — actual date, not "April"] · [Link](url)

**Type:** [News / Sale / Listing / Architecture / Restoration / Market]

**Intensity:** [Quick mention | Worth your POV]

[2–4 sentences on what happened. Concrete. Numbers where they exist — price, sqft, neighborhood, year built, architect. The property is the protagonist. No fluff.]

**Why it's interesting:** [1–2 sentences. The angle that makes this worth Joe's attention. What does it say about the market, the building, the neighborhood, the deal mechanics, the design moment?]

**Use it as:**

- [Concrete suggestion — newsletter angle, social post, quick conversation drop, or something else specific. "Newsletter material" is not concrete; "could anchor a piece on the gap between asks and trades on Bank Street" is.]

- [Optional second angle, only if it's genuinely distinct and strong. Do not pad.]

- [Optional, occasional: **Podcast guest:** [name + one-line on why they'd be a strong guest]. Skip this bullet by default; include only when a person genuinely fits and Joe might want the idea later.]

---

[Repeat for each story.]
Notes on the output
	•	Headline: Joe's framing, property-first. Not "Pop star buys West Village townhouse" — write "1849 Greek Revival on Bank Street trades at $70M after Stern combine."
	•	Intensity: Be honest. Most weeks, most stories are "Quick mention." A few will warrant POV. If everything is "Worth your POV" the label has no signal.
	•	Why it's interesting: This is where the skill earns its keep. A summary of the article is not interesting — Joe could read the article. The value is the take.
	•	Use it as: Make it specific. Always at least one bullet. Add the podcast-guest bullet when there's a plausible guest.
	•	Length discipline: The whole digest should be readable in 5 minutes. If it's longer, cut.
	•	Digest is the deliverable. Editorial process notes (what you searched, what you dropped and why, where the stance was hard to apply) belong in a separate file, not at the bottom of the digest. The digest itself is what Joe reads and forwards.
Voice
Match the project's voice from brain.md:

	•	Professional but warm. Not stiff, not bro-y.
	•	Terse. No padding, no "great question" preamble, no "in conclusion."
	•	Contractions are fine. Em dashes are fine.
	•	Avoid: "utilize", "at this time", "kindly", "please be advised."
	•	The digest is for Joe to read, not for clients — it can have a point of view and should.
Quality bar
A good digest:

	•	Has only verified, in-window stories
	•	Uses celebrity (when present) as a hook, then delivers an insider take only Joe could give
	•	Treats the property, deal, or design as the protagonist of the story underneath
	•	Has a take, not just a summary
	•	Is fair-housing-clean — no protected-class framing of neighborhoods or buyers
	•	Labels intensity honestly
	•	Offers a second "Use it as" angle when one genuinely exists
	•	Is short enough to read in 5 minutes

A bad digest:

	•	Includes unverified facts or stories the agent didn't actually open
	•	Drops celebrity stories outright instead of reframing them with an insider take
	•	Pads with generic "Market Snapshot" blocks
	•	Characterizes neighborhoods or buyers in protected-class terms
	•	Pretends every story warrants Joe's POV
	•	Forces a second "Use it as" angle when there's only one
	•	Strings together headlines without a take
Edge cases
	•	Slow window: Fewer items is fine — quality is the rule. 3 strong items beats 10 weak ones. If it's genuinely dead, say so and propose 1–2 evergreen angles Joe could write about anyway (a recurring market thread, an anniversary of a notable sale, a landmark dispute that's been quiet but not resolved).
	•	Paywalled story: Note the paywall. Summarize only what's visible. Don't invent body details.
	•	Joe asks for a longer window: Same structure, just more items. Group by week if it helps.
	•	Joe asks for a narrower scope (e.g., "just Park Slope this week"): Same structure, narrow the search. Be explicit if you found nothing.
	•	An out-of-window story is essential context (e.g., a sale that closed two weeks ago but a follow-up filing dropped this week): include the older piece as labeled context, not as one of the digest items.
What this skill does NOT do
	•	Does not draft the newsletter itself — that's a separate piece of work. This skill produces the raw material.
	•	Does not contact anyone, post anything, or send anything. Output stays in the conversation / a saved digest file.
	•	Does not surface non-townhouse content (condos, co-ops, rentals) even if the broader market story is interesting — that belongs in a different skill.
