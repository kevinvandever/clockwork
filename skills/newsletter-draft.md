Newsletter Draft
Joe is a NYC residential real estate agent building an upmarket townhouse practice. The newsletter is the public, recurring expression of his brand — it speaks to the ~20K NYC townhouse owners + their advisors and orbit. This skill takes one anchor story and produces a tight draft Joe can revise and ship.

The deliverable is a draft, never a send. Joe reviews and decides what goes out.
When to use this skill
Trigger when Joe asks for:

	•	"Write this week's newsletter"
	•	"Draft a newsletter on the [Bank Street / Cobble Hill / Heights] piece"
	•	"Turn this story into a newsletter"
	•	"Write a take on [property / sale / restoration]"
	•	"Make something out of this" (when paired with a story)

Trigger conditions are met whenever there's a single anchor story and Joe wants long-form public-facing copy from it.

Do NOT use for:

	•	Cold/warm prospect outreach (use personal-sends)
	•	Client or counterparty email (use email-writer)
	•	Listing copy (use listing-description when built)
	•	Multi-story roundup format — this skill is single-anchor on purpose
The job — what a good piece does
The single hardest thing about real estate content is that almost all of it is bad — brochure-y, voiceless, transactional, or celebrity-gossip with no operator underneath. The arbitrage available is voice + substance. The reader is an intelligent, busy townhouse owner or someone in their orbit who is over-served by photography and under-served by anyone with a real point of view about the asset they own.

A good piece does five things:

	•	Leads with the property, not the personality. Even when a celebrity is the hook, the building is the story. (See the editorial stance in townhouse-stories/SKILL.md — same logic applies here, with more space to execute.)
	•	Gives a take only an operator could give. Not the summary a journalist would write. Not the appraisal a fan would offer. The thing only someone who works in the asset class daily would notice — price logic, discount-to-ask signal, board dynamics, restoration choices, LPC angle, comp gaps, what the deal mechanics actually say.
	•	Reads in five minutes. 400-700 words is the discipline. Tight enough that every paragraph earns its space.
	•	Has a voice. Identifiable as Joe, not interchangeable with any other agent's content.
	•	Treats the reader as smart. No "as you know," no remedial framing, no explanation of things the audience already understands.
Input contract
What the skill needs from Joe:

	•	Required: one anchor story. This can be a townhouse-stories digest entry (preferred — has structure), a URL + a sentence on why it's interesting, a paste of an article body, or a free-form description. The skill works with whatever it gets.
	•	Optional but useful: Joe's own notes — what he sees in the story that the article doesn't surface, comp data, building intel, an opinion he wants the piece to land. If provided, these become the spine of the value paragraphs.
	•	Optional: target word count within the 400-700 range. Default 550.

If the input is too thin to produce a real take (just a headline, no body, no operator notes), say so. Don't manufacture authority. Ask for the article body or for Joe's notes and stop.
Voice and register
The piece should read like Matt Levine's Money Stuff applied to NYC townhouses, with Air Mail register and AD-grade subject knowledge where the anchor is a building.

What that means concretely:

Money Stuff (mechanic-density, voice second): Levine is not valuable because he has opinions. He is valuable because every paragraph hands the reader an explained mechanic — how a swap works, why a covenant breaks, what a regulator can and cannot do. The voice is the wrapper; the mechanic is the payload. Apply the same model here: every paragraph should hand the reader a piece of working knowledge they could not produce themselves from the article — a comp methodology, a deal mechanic, a cost range, a constraint, a math. The voice should still read as identifiable Joe — slightly dry, slightly amused, never performative, hedges only when hedging is honest — but voice without mechanic is commentary. Footnote-style asides are fine when they earn the parenthetical.

Air Mail (register): NYC cultural-elite literate. Knowing but not vulgar about wealth. Treats culture, design, and money as adjacent. Names things specifically — buildings, blocks, architects, decades. Does not perform connoisseurship; assumes it.

AD Pro / The World of Interiors / older Curbed long-form (subject-matter bar): When the anchor is a building or a restoration, the piece earns its keep with real architectural literacy. Decade, style, architect, materials, restoration choices, landmark status, what's distinctive about the type. If the piece would not survive being read by an AD editor, the substance needs more work.

Anti-references — do not be:

	•	Mansion Global / WSJ Mansion — too plutocrat-coded, vulgar about wealth.
	•	Brochure / luxury agent newsletter content — photography-led, voiceless, transactional.
	•	The Real Deal — trade-publication tone, wrong register for owners.
	•	Brownstoner gossip mode — useful source, wrong voice. Too casual, too tabloid-y.
	•	Generic market-commentary blog — "Brooklyn median inventory is up 0.6%" is a chart in the wrong format. If data matters, it lives inside a building's story.
Structural template
The default shape — modify when the story requires it, but most pieces should fit this:

LEDE (≈75 words)

The hook. Name the property and the surprising thing in the first sentence

or two. No throat-clearing. No "as we head into spring..." No restating the

news the reader already saw. The lede earns the next paragraph or the

reader leaves.

PORTRAIT (≈200 words)

The property, the deal, the relevant context. What it is, what trades,

what's distinctive. Architect, year, style, footprint, last trade, current

trade, ask, price-per-foot if relevant, location specificity. Concrete.

Numbers where they belong. The building is the protagonist; the buyer is

one note in the paragraph, not the headline.

THE VALUE (≈200 words)

The information, math, mechanics, or framework a competitor would

normally charge for, paywall, or only share inside a paid client

conversation. Not the operator's opinion — the operator's working

knowledge made portable. The comp-pull methodology a sober seller would

have run. The math on three years of holding cost when a number anchors

to a stale comp. The deal mechanic the article didn't unpack. The

restoration-cost range a buyer should expect. The LPC constraint that

explains the price. The test: would a competitor gatekeep this? If yes,

it belongs. If no — if it's just a sharp read — it's commentary, and

commentary is a tweet, not a newsletter paragraph. If this section is

commentary, the piece has failed.

CLOSE (≈75 words)

Broaden the lens slightly. What does this say about the block, the

neighborhood, the asset class, the moment? Or land a small, sharp final

turn. Do not summarize. Do not "in conclusion." End on a sentence the

reader could quote.

The template is the default, not a cage. A piece that needs more portrait and less close can have it. A piece where the value is the lede can invert. But the four functions — hook, portrait, value, close — should all be present.
Length discipline
400-700 words. Default 550. The cap is real, not aspirational.

Why: the audience reads in five minutes or doesn't read. Discipline is its own brand signal — every word earned, no padding. Pieces that drift to 1000+ words signal lack of editorial confidence. If a piece genuinely needs more space, that's a sign the substance warrants two pieces, not one bloated one.

Include the word count at the bottom of the draft so Joe can see the discipline at a glance.
Editorial rules
These transfer directly from townhouse-stories/SKILL.md — the digest's editorial law is also the newsletter's law:

	•	Property as protagonist. Celebrity is a hook, not the story. The building leads the headline, the lede, and the structural emphasis.
	•	Insider value, not commentary. What an operator sees that a journalist or fan couldn't surface, and would normally only share inside a paid client conversation. Price logic, deal mechanics, board dynamics, restoration choices, LPC angle, comp signal — handed over as working knowledge, not floated as opinion.
	•	Fair-housing discipline. No characterization of neighborhoods or buyers in protected-class terms. Avoid demographic-by-occupation/lifestyle shorthand too — "creative professionals," "tech bros," "finance types," "academics," "young families." These erode expert positioning even when they aren't strictly protected. Describe behavior or finance ("a buyer who paid all-cash," "design-forward buyer," "trophy buyer") not category.
	•	Data lives inside stories. No standalone "Market Snapshot" blocks. If a number matters, it anchors to a property, person, or deal.
	•	No demographic-by-neighborhood shorthand. Architecture, retail, transit, history — fine. Stand-ins for who's "supposed to" live somewhere — not fine.

Two newsletter-specific additions:

	•	Verify before asserting. If the input lacks a fact (year built, architect, price), do not invent. Either include only what's confirmed or flag the gap and ask Joe. A draft with a wrong fact actively damages positioning.
	•	Operator confidence, not bluster. The piece should sound confident because Joe knows the asset class — not because the prose performs confidence. No "as anyone who knows the West Village can tell you," no "make no mistake."
Anti-patterns — kill on sight
	•	The brochure paragraph. "This stunning, sun-drenched Greek Revival features five fireplaces, original moldings, and a rare double-wide footprint." Listing copy. Wrong skill.
	•	The personality profile. A paragraph about the celebrity buyer's career, their other homes, their net worth. The building is the story; the buyer is one sentence.
	•	The market-snapshot bolt-on. "More broadly, Manhattan townhouse sales are up 12% year-over-year." If a stat earns space, it earns it inside a specific building's story.
	•	The "as you know" preamble. The reader is smart. Don't explain townhouses to townhouse owners.
	•	The "in conclusion" close. Newsletters don't have conclusions; they have endings. Land somewhere; don't summarize.
	•	The hedge-pile. "It could be argued that, in some sense, the market may possibly be shifting." Commit or cut.
	•	The performative wisdom. "This deal teaches us that real estate is, above all, about people." It doesn't, and it isn't.
	•	The sharp read with no portable value. A clever observation the reader cannot apply. "The trophy market is recalibrating" is a tweet, not a newsletter paragraph. Every paragraph either teaches a mechanic, explains a number with the math behind it, or hands over a method — or it cuts. The gatekeep test: would a competitor paywall this, or would they tweet it for free?
	•	The filler transition. "That said..." "Of course..." "It's worth noting..." Each one earns its keep or gets cut.
	•	Words and phrases on the brain.md never-list. "Utilize," "at this time," "kindly," "please be advised," "circle back," "I'd love to," "super excited," "awesome."
Output structure
Return a single Markdown draft using this shape:

# [Headline — property-first, 8-15 words]

_[Optional dek — one sentence that frames the angle. Skip if the headline does the work.]_

[Lede paragraph]

[Portrait — 1-2 paragraphs]

[Value — 1-2 paragraphs]

[Close paragraph]

---

**Word count:** [number]

**Editor's notes** _(for Joe — strip before publishing)_:

- [Any judgment calls made: what got emphasized, what was left out, what's inferred vs. verified]

- [Any facts the source didn't confirm and that Joe should verify before sending]

- [Optional: alternative angle considered and rejected, with one-line why]
Notes on the output
	•	Headline: Property-first framing. Not "Pop star buys West Village townhouse" — "1849 Greek Revival on Bank Street trades at $70M, and the discount is the story." 8-15 words. Specificity beats cleverness.
	•	Dek: Optional. Use it only when the headline can't carry the angle alone.
	•	Editor's notes: Brief, honest, useful. This is where the drafter flags inference and gaps so Joe doesn't have to reverse-engineer them.
	•	No images. Newsletter publishing decisions are downstream; the draft is text only.
Before returning the draft
Quick checks the drafter should make before handing off:

	•	Word count is in 400-700. (If 700+, cut. If <400, the substance is thin — say so to Joe.)
	•	Lede earns the next paragraph. No throat-clearing.
	•	The value section is portable, not commentary. It hands the reader a mechanic, a method, a number with the math behind it, or a framework — something they could not produce themselves from the article.
	•	Every paragraph passes the gatekeep test — would a competitor paywall this, or would they tweet it for free? If the latter, cut or rewrite.
	•	Property leads the headline and the lede.
	•	No brochure paragraphs, no personality profiles, no market-snapshot bolt-ons.
	•	No fair-housing-risky language, no demographic shorthand.
	•	No phrases on the never-list (brain.md).
	•	Facts are either verified by the input or flagged in Editor's notes.

If any check fails, fix before returning. The draft Joe sees should already pass these.

When the input is wrong for this skill
Refuse cleanly in these cases:

	•	The story is a generic market roundup with no anchor property. This skill is single-anchor by design. Tell Joe and propose either a specific story from the digest or a different format.
	•	The anchor is a celebrity with the property as backdrop. If the building isn't the story, the piece can't be the right voice. Ask Joe whether there's a property-first angle he wants pulled out.
	•	Substance is too thin to support a real take. A two-line article snippet with no body. Ask for more — the article body, Joe's operator notes, or both.

A refusal early is better than a weak draft that erodes the brand.
